Ruthlessly Small

The following was originally written in 2002-2003, I’ve retrieved it from the Internet Archive copy of my original wiki page and lightly edited for formatting with a little updating. I’m reposting it, because I was reminded of it during Damian’s talk at Intersections in Sustainability:

For some reason, I currently find myself fascinated with the concept of the “ruthlessly small”. I first came across the phrase on sohodojo in the context of businesses that could derive a competitive advantage from their very smallness as opposed to the economies of scale that larger businesses can draw upon. This always provokes “David vs Goliath” images in my mind. Paul Graham has written a couple of articles incorporating a similar theme, these are Beating The Averages and The Other Road Ahead.

I am very interested in the applicability of these ideas in business. However, as a software developer and technologist, I am also interested in ruthlessly small software, typically compact enough to fit on a floppy, and ideally written in minimalist programming languages. Here are some examples:

  • Operating systems – Leaf, Puppy Linux, Slitaz
  • Programming Languages – Euphoria, Lua, Rebol and Haxe
  • Applications – see TinyApps

One ruthlessly small business model for a software developer is that of shareware. Programmed for Success is an article that looks at shareware developers, specifically a one man band called Zoot Software.

Another ruthlessly small business model for a software developer is to target a niche such as mobile Java devices. Sell your MobileJava apps at the Nokia Software Market or Handango.

It seems in every Art form, there comes a time when there is a movement towards minimalism. Art Deco in the 20s, painting in the 60’s. Now I would like to make the bold prediction that minimalism will be picked up as the newest trend in programming. I have been a great believer in minimalism (which does have some fundamental differences from eXtreme and agile programming) as I have seem numerious benefits derived from it over a period of 10+ years. Just as sadness is needed to be able to feel happiness, minimalism in programming requires the currently grossly oversized atrocities that consume horrid amounts of disk space. This is one reason why my favorite computing quote is “Inside of every large program is a smaller program struggling to get out.” How many of us can argue with Martin Fowler.

KirkPepperdine

A new example of a successful ruthlessly small software development outfit is Caucho, the developers of the Resin range of Java application servers. According to this article, it is essentially a two man firm.

Andy Singleton of Assembla says in the article that “Caucho is an example of a business that I would call long, strong, and lean. It’s long because it’s distributed geographically and reaches out to outside service providers. It’s strong because it competes globally, every day, and wins. It’s lean because it has a small team and fixed costs that would qualify as roundoff error on the accounts of its competitors.”
