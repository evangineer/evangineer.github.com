Blueprint for a better world: Beware of common sense

UK’s New Scientist magazine is running a series of articles called Blueprint for a better world looking at various ideas for making the world a better place. Given that this is a specialist topic of mind, I thought it might be interesting to evaluate them.

The first article Beware of common sense makes the case for evidence-based government. Given that it has been a number of centuries since The Enlightenment, if it’s not a standard practice by now, there is clearly something wrong with our political cultures. That being the case, perhaps we need to look at the education of public servants including politicians and policy-makers, ensuring that they have enough of an indoctrination into the Scientific Method that evidence-based government is the rule rather than the exception.

An alternative and perhaps more radical move is specifically create a place for scientists and engineers in our democratic structures. So alongside the House of Commons and House of Lords, there would be a House of Technocrats! Alongside Congress and the Senate, the Academy. That’s not to say there aren’t deeper problems with our democracies that won’t be solved by this. There are, but at least this would embed an evidence-based approach more deeply into our democratic structures.

Cloudy Horizons for Developers

The following is in part a response to Directgov Innovate’s Prototyping in the Cloud blog post & something of a summary of my own views on the cloud as a web developer.

Cloud hosting as described by the aforementioned post, is what’s known as Infrastructure as a Service. The most well known available services along these lines are Amazon Web Services like EC2 and S3. Another service along those lines is Mosso also known as the Rackspace Cloud. The open source equivalent of AWS is Eucalyptus which is being incorporated into the Ubuntu Server release. Having looked at the APIs for the new Cloud offering from Sun, it fits into this category of cloud hosting.

Speaking as a web developer, I’ve gotten tired of wrestling with system administration issues when deploying/installing web applications. Anything that helps ease deployment removes hassle from the process & allows me to spend my time on more useful activities. There are developer oriented deployment tools like Capistrano and Fabric that can be helpful in this regards.

In the context of cloud computing, a solution for taking the system administration hassle out of deployment is what’s called Platform as a Service. The most notable examples of PaaS from my perspective are Google App Engine and Heroku. The reasons they are notable in my view is that Django & other Python web developers can deploy modified versions of their apps onto App Engine and Rails & other Ruby web developers can deploy their apps onto Heroku. Force.com from salesforce.com is also in the PaaS category.

An emergent trend that I’ve found interesting regarding PaaS is the rise of Server-Side Javascript in a break from established server-side dynamic languages like PHP, Perl, Python and Ruby. You see this in Appjet, 10gen and Reasonably Smart Platform. Whereas Appjet is exclusively Javascript based, 10gen and Reasonably Smart are in various stages of incorporating support for Python and Ruby amongst others.

10gen’s application server Babble is written in Java, with the Javascript support courtesy of Rhino. Support for Python and Ruby is being integrated via Jython and JRuby respectively. Reasonably Smart Platform is written in Perl, with Javascript via Spidermonkey. The intention is eventually to harness the IronMonkey project to integrate Python and Ruby via IronPython and IronRuby.

10gen and Reasonably Smart have announced major changes this year. 10gen is shifting emphasis to its cloud database MongoDB based on demand for related support & services and has changed the open source license on Babble to the Apache license in order to invite greater engagement from open source developers. Reasonably Smart has been acquired by the major hosting provider Joyent who have an IaaS offering called Accelerators.

So all that aside, what do I recommend to Directgov Innovate? 1) Use Amazon Web Services for IaaS cloud hosting. Issues have been raised about data protection. Amazon has European AWS farms that you can specifically use to avoid the data leaving the EU. 2) Look at ways of easing deployment for developers, possibly by leveraging deployment tools like Capistrano & Fabric making it easier for developers using major web frameworks like Django & Rails, alternatively incorporating open source PaaS on top of AWS or by implementing multitenant environments like Tyrant. 3) Hire someone to actually work out the details & work with you to implement it!

The Economies of Agility and Disrupting the Nature of the Firm

In the industrial age, mass production by firms exploited the economies of scale. We are however transitioning to a postindustrial age, the network age where emerging Peer Production will be driven by the economies of agility. Economies of scale are about driving down costs of manufactured goods by producing them on a large scale. Economies of agility in contrast are about quickly being able to switch between producing different goods and services in response to demand. We see an early variant of this in Dell’s build to order model which disrupted the Personal Computer industry.

So what is going on here? Thanks to technological developments, we are increasingly able to produce more sophisticated goods and services without being in firms. As the Nobel prize winning economist Ronald Coase showed, firms exist to reduce transaction costs and organize production. In a world where there are alternative means of radically reducing transaction costs and organizing production via the Internet, firms will become increasingly irrelevant and will either change radically or cease to exist. In their stead, will emerge networks of individuals & small groups collaborating to produce goods and services that rival anything produced by large corporations. We already see this happening in the area of free and open source software, with GNU/Linux challenging Microsoft Windows, also in Mozilla Firefox vs Microsoft Internet Explorer. This has been dubbed Commons-based Peer Production by Harvard law professor Yochai Benkler author of The Wealth of Networks. Another example of commons-based peer production competing with & beating corporate rivals is Wikipedia which has already seen off Microsoft Encarta & has Encyclopaedia Brittanica well in its sights.

How far can this go? Nobody knows. However, there are now serious investigations going on into peer producing human rights! Perhaps this is the development that might help bring the Universal Declaration of Human Rights to the majority of the world’s population.

Economías de Comunidad Local para la Seguridad en un Mundo Inestable

Contents
  • 1 Acknowledgment
  • 2 La Globalización y las Comunidades Locales
  • 3 Capas de Economías
  • 4 Identificar las Funciones Clave del Intercambio Local
  • 5 ¿Qué Tipo de Intercambio Local?
  • 6 Potenciar la Economía Local con la Tecnología

1  Acknowledgment

Kindly translated to spanish by Lucas Gonzalez from Local Community Economics for Security in an Unstable World

2  La Globalización y las Comunidades Locales

La globalización ha traído muchos beneficios en términos de precio y disponibilidad de bienes y servicios, intercambio con el extranjero, etc. También ha traído costos en términos de cadenas de suministro alargadas, deslocalización de los puestos de trabajo, peligros para la seguridad del suministro alimentario, exposición a los remolinos económicos globales, etc. Una cierta cantidad de re-localización puede actuar como un colchón para proteger a la comunidad de los efectos adversos de la globalización al tiempo que les permite recoger sus beneficios. Dependiendo de cómo se haga la re-localización, la comunidad puede volverse más resistente, auto-suficiente, sostenible y paradójicamente más capaz de atraer al comercio y las inversiones del exterior debido al aumento local del capital social y del capital de conocimientos.

3  Capas de Economías

Una forma de mirar a las economías es considerarlas como similares a la atmósfera con diferentes capas que manifiestasn propiedades variables con cierto grado de solapamiento y fluidez: así que tenemos la economía global en la que la moneda de reserva dominante era el petrodólar, pero en la que parece haber una transición al euro; está la economía nacional en la que las transacciones de mercado se realizan sobre todo en la moneda que sea de curso legal [“legal tender”]; la economía personal o “nuclear” que opera en el interior de la familia, entre amigos y vecinos y en la que la moneda de cambio son los favores.

Las comunidades a menudo pueden tener problemas debidos al flujo insuficiente de dinero procedente de las economías nacionales y globales. Esto puede aliviarse estableciendo una nueva capa económica, una economía local basada en la creación de una forma local de intercambio que movilice la capacidad local sobrante, que existe en forma de destrezas y recursos, de manera eficaz en un área en la que la gente, por otro lado, es pobre en dinero y rica en tiempo. El dinero tiene muchas funciones, y una que es clave es ser un sistema de información que lleva la contabilidad de la asignación de servicios y recursos.

4  Identificar las Funciones Clave del Intercambio Local

Cualquier forma de intercambio local que incorpore y optimice la función de sistema de información del dinero tendrá un impacto positivo mucho mayor en la economía local que una que no lo haga. De hecho, podríamos hablar de [personas] ricas en información y pobres en información; sin embargo, en el contexto de la economía local, en lo que estamos interesados específicamente es en las ofertas y necesidades, destrezas y recursos. Un recurso podría ser un terreno, un taladro o una habitación, por ejemplo. Hay muchas destrezas diferentes en cualqueir comunidad, pero todas se agrupan en tres categorías principales: destrezas con las cosas, con la información y con la gente. La riqueza real de una comunidad está en las destrezas y recursos disponibles para la gente que hay en ella. Un sistema de información bien diseñado no sólo puede hacer que esta riqueza sea visible, sino que también puede liberarla para el mayor beneficio de la comunidad y de todos los que viven en ella.

El dinero como deuda/crédito conduce a otro aspecto dle ecosistema que hay en torno al intercambio: la confiabilidad de las partes implicadas en un intercambio. En las economías nacionales y globales, aquí es donde entran en acción las clasificaciones de los créditos. Una forma de intercambio local que incorporase nociones relacionadas con la confianza y la reputación sería más robusta que una que no lo hiciese.

5  ¿Qué Tipo de Intercambio Local?

La forma más antigua de intercambio, que existió antes que el dinero, fue el trueque. El intercambio directo de bienes por otros bienes entre dos partes también conocido como trasposición. Sin embargo esto no puede hacerse a gran escala debido al tiempo y a la complejidad implicaods en hacer transacciones de trueque más complejas. La dificultad y costo de las transacciones de trueque complejas condujo a la invención de medios de intercambio que evolucionarían hasta convertirse en las monedas a las que nos referimos como dinero. El punto clave aquí es que el dinero se usa para mediar en las transacciones. Sin embargo, el papel del dinero aquí puede ser reemplazado por un sistema de ifnroamción que se construye con el propósito de facilitar y posibilitar el trueque complejo multi-agente tanto directamente como indirectamente a través de lo que podría llamarse una moneda local o complementaria. Una versión establecida y altamente exitosa del trueque es la usada por los negocios y que funciona a escala global es la BarterCard [TarjetaDeTrueque].

Otra forma de intercambio es tratar al Tiempo como Dinero e intercambiar horas de servicio por recursos y/u otros servicios. Hay muchos modelos en todo el mundo basados en esta forma de intercambio tales como los Bancos de tiempo, los Dólares de tiempo y las Horas Ítaca [Timebanks, Timedollars, Ithaca Hours]. Dentro de los círculos de los bancos de tiempo, se toma como principio que el tiempo es lo más adaptado como forma de intercambio en la economía personal y en la porción basada en servicios de la economía local.

Una forma de intercambio que también se usa en un número de modelos es el crédito mutuo en el que se confeccionan “pagarés” personales en los que figura una cantidad en moneda local y que se intercambian por bienes y servicios. Hay muchos modelos en todo el mundo que operan sobre este principio incluyendo varios sistemas de Intercambio y Comercio Local [“Local Exchange and Trading Systems”, denominados LETS] y las Berkshares.

Los sistemas mencionados son las formas de intercambio alternativas más conocidas y comprendidas además de los vales y los sistemas de lealtad tales como los Vales de Regalo de iTunes, las Tesco ClubCard y las Air Miles.

6  Potenciar la Economía Local con la Tecnología

Podemos hacer uso de la web y de los teléfonos móviles para hacer posibles los intercambios locales para beneficio de la economía local creando un sistema que lleve la contabilidad, la correduría y otros tipos de funciones “de sistema de información” que normalmente son hechas posibles por el dinero y el ecosistema que hay a su alrededor. Ya hay un número de sistemas disponibles para hacer esto con varios grados. Sin embargo, no hay ninguno que pueda facilitar la escala y la calidad de colaboración necesaria para asegurar que la economía local no sólo sobreviva sino que prospere y posibilite que la comunidad desarrolle de manera resiliente y sostenible que pueda atraer el comercio y la inversión externos incluso en condiciones económicas globales adversas.

Un sistema así apoyaría a la comunidad para en su toma de decisiones en conjunto o como grupos más pequeños tal como fuese apropiado. Apoyaría la gestión de proyectos y de tareas para el beneficio colectivo de la comunidad así como de subconjuntos de la misma. Apoyaría la agregación de destrezas y recursos, y luego ayudaría en su gestión. Usando herramientas tales como mapas y calendarios haría más fácil encontrar bienes, servicios y recursos disponibles y participar en el intercambio apropiado de los mismos, incluyendo cuando sea apropiado el uso de dinero como parte del pago permitiendo así que la economía local se conecte de manera más eficiente con las economías nacional y global. Las capacidades de comunicación potentes serían inherentes al sistema.

El resultado final es hacer posible la acción colectiva eficaz a través de la economía local para beneficio de la comunidad y de todos los que son parte de ella aunque sean pobres en dinero y ricos en tiempo. Hacerlo así libera la riqueza real de las comunidades que las economias nacional y global a menudo pasan por alto.

Local Community Economics for Security in an Unstable World

Contents
  • 1 Globalisation & Local Communities
  • 2 Layers of Economies
  • 3 Identifying Key Functions of Local Exchange
  • 4 What Kind of Local Exchange?
  • 5 Enabling the Local Economy with Technology

1  Globalisation & Local Communities

Globalisation has brought many benefits in terms of price & availability of goods & services, foreign exchange etc.. It has also brought costs in terms of lengthened supply chains, job offshoring, compromised food security, exposure to global economic turmoil etc.. A certain amount of re-localisation can act as a buffer to protect a community from the adverse effects of globalisation while still enabling them to reap the benefits from it. Depending on how the re-localisation is done, the community can become more resilient, self-sufficient, sustainable and paradoxically better able to attract external trade and investment due to the increase in local social and knowledge capital.

2  Layers of Economies

One way of looking at economies is to consider them to be similar to the atmosphere with different layers which exhibit variying properties with some degree of overlap and fluidity: so we have the global economy where the dominant reserve currency was the petrodollar, but a transition to the Euro seems to be underway; there’s the national economy where market transactions are conducted primarily in the currency which is legal tender; the personal or “core” economy which operates amongst family, friends and neighbours where the currency is favours.

Communities can often have problems due to insufficient inflow of money from the national and global economies. This can be alleviated by establishing a new economic layer, a local economy based on the creation of a local form of exchange which mobilizes local spare capacity in the form of skills and resources effectively in an area where people are otherwise money-poor and time-rich. Money has many functions, one of the key ones being an information system that brokers the allocation of services and resources.

3  Identifying Key Functions of Local Exchange

Any form of local exchange that incorporates and optimizes the information system function of money will have a much greater positive impact on the local economy then one that does not. In fact, we could talk about information-rich vs information-poor, however in the context of a local economy, what we are specifically interested in are offers and wants, skills and resources. A resource could be a field, a drill or a room for example. There are many different skills existent in any community, but they will fall under three main categories, skills with things, with information and with people. The real wealth of a community lies in the skills and resources available to the people in it. A well-designed information system can not only make this wealth visible, it can liberate it for the greater benefit of the community and all who live in it.

Money as Debt/Credit leads to another aspect of the ecosystem around exchange, the trustworthiness of the parties involved in an exchange. In national and global economies, this is where credit ratings come into play. A form of local exchange that incorporated notions related to trust and reputation would be more robust than one that didn’t.

4  What Kind of Local Exchange?

The oldest form of exchange, one that predated the invention of money is barter.The direct exchange of goods for other goods between two parties otherwise known as swapping. However this does not scale due to the time and complexity involved in doing more complex barter transactions. The difficulty and cost of complex barter transactions led to the invention of mediums of exchange that would evolve into the currencies that we refer to as money. The key point here is that money is used to mediate transactions. However, the role of money here can be replaced by an information system that is purpose built to facilitate and enable complex multi-party barter either directly or indirectly through what could be called a local or complementary currency. One highly successful, established version of barter that is used by business and operates on a global scale is BarterCard

Another form of exchange is to treat Time As Money and exchange hours of service for resources and/or other services. There are many schemes around the world based on this form of exchange such as Timebanks, Timedollars and Ithaca Hours. Within timebanking circles, it is a tenet that time is most suited as a form of exchange in the personal economy and within the service-based portion of the local economy.

A form of exchange that is also used in a number of schemes is mutual credit where personally issued IOUs denominated in a local currency are exchanged for goods and services. There are many schemes around the world operating on this principle including various Local Exchange and Trading Systems (aka LETS) and Berkshares.

The aforementioned are the most popular and understood alternative forms of exchange besides vouchers and loyalty schemes such as iTunes Gift Vouchers, Tesco ClubCard and Air Miles.

5  Enabling the Local Economy with Technology

We can make use of the web and mobile phones to enable local exchanges for the benefit of the local economy by creating a system that does the accounting, brokerage and other “information system” type functions that are normally enabled by money and the ecosystem around it. There are already a number of systems available to do this to varying degrees. However, there are none available that can facilitate the scale and quality of collaboration needed to ensure that the local economy not only survives but thrives and enables the community to develop in a resilient, sustainable way that can attract external trade and investment even in adverse global economic conditions.

Such a system would support the community to make decisions as a whole or as smaller groups as appropriate. It would support the management of projects and tasks for the collective benefit of the community as well as subsets of it. It would support the pooling of skills and resources, then helping with the management of them. Utilizing tools like maps and calendars would make it easier to find available goods, services and resources and engage in appropriate exchanges of them, including where appropriate the use of money in part payment thus enabling the local economy to more efficiently connect to the national and global economies. Powerful communications capabilities would be inherent in the system.

The bottom line is enabling effective collective action via the local economy for the benefit of the community and all who are part of it even though they may be money poor and time rich. Doing so unleashes the true wealth of communities that the national and global economies all often overlook.

The Press Leads The Public – The Nightly News and the Agenda-Setting Role of the Corporate News Media

Apparently the Japanese have a saying, “the press leads the public” which refers to the agenda-setting role of the mass news media. Bernard C. Cohen put it another way, “The press may not be successful much of the time in telling people what to think, but it is stunningly successful in telling its readers what to think about.” In other words, the mass media creates a conceptual box which defines the issues that the majority of the general public think about and discuss. This box tends to be constrained by the corporate agenda imposed by the entities that own the mass media. The long-term effects of this is cultural and political homogenization, plus social complacency bordering on stasis. This is a recipe for a sick, maladaptive society and culture.

I came across the Japanese saying while reading Jonathan Hickman’s work The Nightly News which is a remarkable polemic on the corporate-owned news media which is best described by Andy Diggle as “It’s Network meets Fight Club and it’s the best new comic of the year.” Each issue/chapter of The Nightly News has a Credits listing at the end where Hickman points out the references that he used. A couple of the Must Read references are Hard News by Seth Mnookin and The Underground History of American Education by John Taylor Gatto.

Besides the subject matter, another thing that makes The Nightly News extraordinary is the unprecedented level of graphic design that has never been seen before in the comics/graphic novel medium. This includes the use of infographics to illustrate factual data on matters such as the increasing concentration of mass media ownership into six global corporations, the fabrication of news stories in major news outlets (e.g. Rathergate) and the growing use of Ritalin to modify the behavior of young boys in the USA. Two design portals that Hickman has acknowledged being influenced by are Kaliber10000 and DesignChapel.

Given the apparent biased and narrow news coverage of the corporate owned mainstream media which has been pointed out elsewhere, what is the thoughtful citizen to do? One thing is to regularly read websites that set out to critique and hold to account the media and its public relations symbiote such as medialens, Media Channel, Spinwatch and Center for Media and Democracy. Another is to seek out alternative news sources to broaden the range of news, analysis and opinion that one is exposed to such as open Democracy News Analysis, IPS Inter Press Service, Worldchanging and News Alternative.

In conclusion, the corporate owned mainstream media should be regarded as just one portion of the balanced media diet and not even the dominant portion at that. Critical reasoning and systems thinking should be applied to all news, analysis and opinion regardless of where it originates from. Those skills should be taught to children at the earliest stage, their cognitive development reasonably allows.

Specialisation is for insects?

Those who spend sufficient time around me will have heard me quoting Robert Heinlein’s line about specialisation being for insects. According to the New York Times Economix blog that may not even be true for insects.

There are some thoughtful opposing views in the comments on that blog. I think though that Heinlein has a point which has also been made by radical educator John Taylor Gatto many times in his talks and writings including The Guerilla Curriculum – How to Get An Education in Spite of School and Confederacy of Dunces – The Tyranny of Compulsory Schooling.

From a Transition Towns relocalization perspective, it isn’t necessary that everybody in the local community should each have the full range of skills discussed by Heinlein and Gatto but that the skills should be sufficiently available in the locality and the relevant raw materials needed should also be locally available. Heinlein is describing an extreme of self-sufficiency and our current global trade-based consumerist economy is an extreme of dependency on materials and skills being available on what could be the other side of the world. A more optimal balance is going to be somewhere in between with the proviso that most basic needs should be met locally to be sustainable in the face of massive oil shocks or a full blown peak oil scenario. Dr Helen has some thoughts about disaster preparation which elaborate on this. If you think that’s over the top, remember Hurricane Katrina. Couldn’t happen in London, you say. You’re probably right, but the Thames Flood Barrier does have a reason to exist and rising sea levels due to global warming could render it obsolete over time.

Personal Data Tracking

An emergent form of website is one that like Daytum and Mycrocosm allows users to track and visualize personal data that can be as trivial as how many cups of coffee they have during the day and or as critical as how many times they have taken their heart medicine.

Out of the various classes of data that can be tracked, it seems to me the ones most worthwhile are to do with wellbeing, finances and ecological footprint. In order to reduce what might otherwise be an onerous data entry burden, a dedicated client that runs on the user’s mobile phone and uploads data to the website via its api. An interesting twist could be to not only track the good habits that a user is trying to cultivate but also to prompt the user to perform the actions that the good habits consist of.

Ruthlessly Small Reloaded

Contents
  • 1 Ruthlessly Small

The following was originally written in 2002-2003, I’ve retrieved it from the Internet Archive copy of my original wiki page and lightly edited for formatting with a little updating. I’m reposting it, because I was reminded of it during Damian’s talk at Intersections in Sustainability:

1  Ruthlessly Small

For some reason, I currently find myself fascinated with the concept of the “ruthlessly small”. I first came across the phrase on sohodojo in the context of businesses that could derive a competitive advantage from their very smallness as opposed to the economies of scale that larger businesses can draw upon. This always provokes “David vs Goliath” images in my mind. Paul Graham has written a couple of articles incorporating a similar theme, these are Beating The Averages and The Other Road Ahead.

Intersections in Sustainability

Last Thursday, I attended the Intersections in Sustainability event organized by Create KX. The venue is The Hub King’s Cross. It’s the first time, I’ve been there since they finished the renovations. I’ve been there before when it was a newly acquired disused site and the difference is night and day. The place is like something out of Grand Designs with quite stunning interiors.

The event was facilitated by Tommy Hutchinson of i-genius

First speaker was James Wallbank, an artist and free technology activist who also directs the art & technology group Redundant Technology Initiative, and is the CEO of Access Space. He spoke about his view that technology is changing society. Sheffield in the mid-90s was in poor shape with a low level of ICT skills. James as an unemployed artist, decided to take the computers that were being skipped and put them to cool, creative use. Wound up with nearly 2,500 computers. More computers than they know what to do with. Founded Access Space in 2000 as a open access space in order to find ways of using those computers.

Reusing is better than recycling, but requires a high level of skill. Treats visitors not as customers but as participants. Encourages them to collaborate, teach and support each other. James believes in harnessing collective intelligence and teaching people how to participate in a community again as the majority of us in this society have never learned about how to truly be in community. One key is being positive with affirming creative contributions, while shunning takers.

James says that it’s about everything, he’s had to consider every aspect of what he does, not just financially but also socially and otherwise. A future way of business may be that everything is important.

Starting to engage with the idea of ICT for Development. In the meantime, they are pursuing two initiatives:

  • Grow Your Own Medialab – helping others setup something like Access Space.
  • Zero Dollar Laptop – Taking disused dust-gathering laptops, making them functional and giving them away to people who can make use of the renovated machines.

My thoughts: Q: What’s the relationship between the Redundant Technology Initiative and Remade in Brixton? Can we grow a set of connected youth-focused medialabs around South London.

The next speaker Damian Kimmelman is director and developer at Vivid Image. He questioned the idea of necessity being the mother of invention. He spoke about applying design principles such as building websites according to the lowest common denominator in terms of computer capabilities using graceful degradation. They aren’t necessarily constraints on creativity, but may instead be the launch pad for it. An example he gave was of streaming video without buffering on low bandwidth internet connections.

He gave examples of ways in which technologies are being re-purposed in a multitude of new and unforseen ways:

  • iPhones and Wii’s both make heavy and innovative use of accelerometers for motion detection. Acclerometers have been around for years but are new to consumer electronics.
  • Creating heart organs using printers, sounds unbelievable doesn’t it?
  • Transferring airtime minutes between villages in Africa using mobile phones. Turning airtime into a new form of currency. I do believe something like that is happening in Kenya.
  • Anti-spam technology in the form of Recaptcha being used to digitize books and newspapers in a similar fashion to Amazon Mechanical Turk.

Damian was clearly put off his stride by being unable to show the relevant web pages from his laptop. The Hub has invested in a projector that apparently only works with Apple laptops with DVI connectors. James was also unable to hook up his laptop but displayed what he had to show via the third speaker’s laptop.

Graham Harwood being the third speaker in question sported an Apple laptop which worked with the projector but not with the Hub’s audio setup, so when he showed his video, there was no soundtrack to go with it. So Harwood spoke about The MediaShed, a free media space setup by the now defunct Mongrel artists group in Southend. Mediashed is about free media in order to replace money with imagination. Harwood defines free media in similar terms to free software as described by Richard Stallman.

One of the projects undertaken by the MediaShed was video sniffing in shops. Apparently you can take one of the video senders used to retransmit multi-channel TV around the house and use it to pick up footage from CCTV cameras.

Having proven the concept, MediaShed went to the Manchester Arndale Shoppng Centre, and used the inhouse CCTV for filming two free runners doing their thing over three nights in the centre. The resulting film was shown for several days on the big plasma screens at the Arndale.

One of Harwood’s Interactive Media M.A. students at Goldsmith’s is going beyond Video Sniffing to Signal Hijacking sending games and videos to surveillance cameras using a modified variant of video sending technology.

A number of other projects mentioned by Harwood are Antarctic Data Jam, Ambient Weather Sound Machine, and Social Telephony, kind of like a text to audio twitter on the radio.

One example of Social Telephony was A Roundhead using Oliver Cromwell as a persona for a mental hospital. The voice of Cromwell would call in various messages to staff at the hospital. Apparently this was successful and well received by both staff and management. For a London-based Congolese refugee community radio programme on Resonance FM, they did Telephone Trottoire. The refugees are displaced due to conflicts over coltan, a source of tantalum which is used for mobile phones. Almon Strowger invented the switch for direct dial. Harwood and his colleagues have reconstructed six Strowger switches in San Jose as part of a Tantalum Memorial.

For the next Tantalum Memorial, they are according to Harwood reanimating twenty six switches for the Science Museum in an upcoming exhibition in the near future. One key theme of the work by Harwood and the MediaShed is our apparent transition from a society that imprisons the body to one that controls us and curtails our freedoms.

All in all much food for thought from all the speakers, with new ideas and possibilities for collaboration afoot.

F.I.A.S.C.O. 2008

I’m part way through F.I.A.S.C.O. which is Frank Partnoy’s account of life as a derivative salesman in Morgan Stanley’s Derivatives Planning Group. It’s a fascinating and at times borderline horrific insider’s view of how derivatives are created and sold. Regarding one particular structured note that was quite successful for Morgan Stanley (PERLS), he identifies two categories of buyers, “cheaters” and “widows and orphans”. “Widows and orphans” are buyers such as local authorities/municipalities, insurance companies and schools who typically have no idea what a yield curve is, and therefore have no real idea of the risk/reward equation involved in buying a derivative. Therefore it is extremely likely that they will be missold a derivative with a far higher risk than they are aware of by a salesman who is “ripping the face off” of his woefully ignorant clients.

So why did I explain the above. Partnoy is describing what was already an established practice by 1993. There have been allegations that Partnoy has made exaggerations and offers little evidence to backup what he says. Be that as it may, this New York Times article describes how one particular set of widows and orphans, a group of Wisconsin school districts were persuaded to borrow the best part of $200 million and invest it in collataralized debt obligations (the very same credit derivatives at the heart of the credit crunch). Reading the story, it is clear that they and the New York subway authorities have had their faces ripped off and are now suffering the resulting pain. How many other widows and orphans are out there with ripped off faces and what will the implications be in terms of their being able to meet their financial obligations such as paying salaries and pensions, not to mention cover their operating costs. Budget cuts and job losses for schools, hospitals and local public services around the world but particularly in North America and Western Europe seem inevitable as a result of this.

One key reform that policymakers may want to consider in the light of this are banning unregulated over the counter derivatives and instead only allowing properly regulated exchange-traded derivatives, so that there is greater visibility about what is going on. Another more controversial measure is to either nationalize the credit ratings agencies or break them up applying reforms to ensure that they aren’t complicit in giving unwarranted ratings to undeserving financial instruments just because they are getting paid to do so by the investment bankers working for the issuer of the instruments. As things stand, credit ratings agencies like auditors are pretty much inherently corrupt because their remuneration and business models incentivizes them to be corrupt. Just as massive cash bonuses based on short-term performances incentivizes investment bankers to engage in lucrative high-risk transactions regardless of the medium/long-term impact on their employers and clients.

Defining the Triple Crunch

Plenty of people must be looking at various crises and be wondering what on earth is going on. In my view, we are facing an almost unprecedented Triple Crunch.

We are all probably sick of hearing about the Credit Crunch_. What’s getting less attention but is still making an impact on our wallets is a food and fuel crunch. This is due to high oil prices which has a domino effect on a system of industrialized food production which is dependent on petrochemicals for fertilizers and distribution to consumers. Andrew Simmscontactdetail.aspx?page=877&folder=142&cid=8 who is the Policy Director of the new economics foundation gives a slightly different definition in his Guardian article:

The triple crunch of a credit-fuelled financial crisis, accelerating climate change and soaring energy prices – how did we get into this mess? In the face of so many simultaneous crises, we all have legitimate questions for the governments that allowed us to sleepwalk into this situation.

However you define it, the majority of people (ie low to middle income earners) have genuine reasons to be gravely concerned on a number of fronts. In doing so, most people are overlooking the opportunity to reconsider what is thought of as “business as usual” in economic and political affairs. The prevailing models of neoliberal capitalism and representative democracy have both failed, just as communism failed with the collapse of the Berlin Wall. We need more just than a Green New Deal but it would certainly be a good start. Rather than repeat past mistakes, how can we move to the next level of human civilisation? One that ensures that everyone has enough to eat, a decent roof over their heads, enough energy for their needs, a proper say in the decisions that affect their lives and the opportunity to reach their full potential to contribute to society.

Given that it is Crunch Time for humanity and the planet, perhaps we should consider Plan B or even Plan C?

PS Looks like a water crunch may be in the works, according to experts attending this year’s Stockholm International Water Conference.
